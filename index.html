Introduction

GlobalXYZ pharmaceutical company has completed a randomized controlled drug trial and provided the dataset to our non-profit organization for independent safety analysis. This report examines the drug's adverse reaction profile to determine if adverse reactions are of significant proportions.

The study follows a 2:1 randomization ratio (Drug:Placebo) and includes comprehensive demographic data, vital signs, and adverse effect monitoring over multiple time points.

Research Questions

  1. Do adverse effect proportions differ significantly between Drug and Placebo?
  2. Is the number of adverse effects independent of treatment group?
  3. Are there age differences between treatment groups?

6. Statistical Test Results

Test 1: Proportion of Adverse Effects Between Groups

Hypothesis:

  • H₀: p₁ = p₂ (proportions are equal)
  • H₁: p₁ ≠ p₂ (proportions differ)

Test Details:

  • Test: Two-sample z-test for proportions
  • Two-tailed test
  • α = 0.05

Results:

Drug Group:
1,568/4,490 = 0.349 (34.9%)
Placebo Group:
703/2,246 = 0.313 (31.3%)
Difference:
3.6 percentage points
P-value (two_sample_p_value): 0.0043
95% CI for difference: [0.011, 0.061]

Interpretation: STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT

  • • We reject the null hypothesis (p = 0.0043 < 0.05)
  • • Drug group has significantly higher adverse effect rate
  • • 95% confident the true difference is between 1.1% and 6.1%
  • • Relative increase: ~11.5% higher in drug group

Test 2: Independence of Number of Adverse Effects and Treatment

Hypothesis:

  • H₀: Number of effects is independent of treatment
  • H₁: Number of effects is associated with treatment

Test Details:

  • Test: Chi-square test of independence
  • Degrees of freedom: 4
  • α = 0.05

Results:

Chi-square statistic:
χ² = 12.84
Degrees of freedom:
df = 4
Effect size:
Cramér's V = 0.044 (small)
P-value (num_effects_p_value): 0.0121
Critical value (α=0.05): 9.488

Interpretation: STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT

  • • We reject the null hypothesis (p = 0.0121 < 0.05)
  • • Number of adverse effects is not independent of treatment
  • • Drug group shows higher tendency for multiple effects
  • • Effect size is small but statistically detectable

Test 3: Age Difference Between Treatment Groups

Hypothesis:

  • H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ (mean ages are equal)
  • H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂ (mean ages differ)

Test Details:

  • Test: Independent samples t-test
  • Two-tailed test
  • α = 0.05

Results:

Drug Group:
Mean = 64.5 years (SD = 9.8)
Placebo Group:
Mean = 64.3 years (SD = 9.9)
Difference:
0.2 years (t = 0.52)
P-value (age_group_effects_p_value): 0.604
95% CI for mean difference: [-0.5, 0.9]

Interpretation: NOT STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT

  • • We fail to reject the null hypothesis (p = 0.604 > 0.05)
  • • No significant difference in mean ages between groups
  • • Groups are well-balanced for age
  • • Confirms successful randomization

Key Takeaways

  • Higher Adverse Effect Rate: Drug 34.9% vs Placebo 31.3% (+3.6 pp)
  • Dose-Response Pattern: Drug users more likely to experience multiple effects
  • Well-Randomized Trial: No age bias between groups
  • Modest Effect Size: Statistically significant but clinically modest differences

Clinical Significance

  • Absolute Risk Increase: 3.6 percentage points
  • Relative Risk Increase: ~11.5%
  • Number Needed to Harm: ~28 patients
  • Clinical Judgment: Risk-benefit assessment needed

8. Conclusions and Recommendations

Primary Conclusions

  1. The drug produces a statistically significant increase in adverse effects vs placebo
  2. The increase is modest (3.6 percentage points, ~11.5% relative increase)
  3. Pattern suggests dose-related or cumulative adverse effects
  4. Trial design was robust with successful randomization

For Clinical Practice

  • • Inform patients of ~35% adverse effect rate
  • • Implement close monitoring protocols
  • • Consider risk stratification for vulnerable populations
  • • Evaluate individual risk-benefit profiles

For Regulatory Consideration

  • • Investigate specific adverse effect types and severity
  • • Assess risk-benefit ratio with efficacy data
  • • Consider subgroup analyses by demographics
  • • Monitor long-term effects beyond 20 weeks

For Further Research

  • • Identify specific adverse effects driving differences
  • • Investigate risk factors for multiple effects
  • • Examine temporal patterns of adverse events
  • • Analyze laboratory correlates (WBC, RBC)

9. Study Limitations

Data Limitations

  • • Missing laboratory data (WBC, RBC) limits analysis
  • • Adverse effects aggregated, preventing specific condition analysis
  • • Limited demographic diversity in study population

Temporal Limitations

  • • 20-week follow-up may miss long-term effects
  • • Cannot assess rare or delayed adverse reactions
  • • Generalizability limited to studied age group (58-72 years)

10. Methodology Notes

Statistical Software

Analysis performed using Python with scipy.stats and pandas libraries

Statistical Tests Used

  • • Two-sample proportion z-test: statsmodels.stats.proportion.proportions_ztest
  • • Chi-square test: scipy.stats.chi2_contingency
  • • Independent t-test: scipy.stats.ttest_ind

Assumptions Verified

  • • Sample sizes sufficient for normal approximation in proportion test
  • • Expected frequencies ≥5 for chi-square test (met)
  • • Approximate normality for t-test (central limit theorem)
  • • Independence of observations assumed from RCT design
